The case for renaissance engineers

The modern world has a specialization problem. We have organized knowledge into departments, disciplines, and domains so narrow that the people best equipped to solve our hardest problems are those who refuse to pick a lane.

A renaissance engineer is someone who builds across boundaries. They may write code and compose music. Design circuits and study philosophy. Run experiments and paint murals. They are not dilettantes—they are synthesizers. They see connections invisible to specialists because they have inhabited multiple worlds and carry each world's language in their thinking.

We believe this way of working is not a relic of the past. It is an urgent necessity for the future. The problems ahead—climate, consciousness, coordination, coexistence—do not respect disciplinary walls.

  1. Depth in multiple domains is not a contradiction; it is a compounding advantage.
  2. The most important ideas live in the gaps between fields.
  3. Building is a form of thinking. Theory without craft is incomplete.
  4. Curiosity is not a phase. It is a practice, and it deserves institutional support.
  5. The world needs people who can hold the whole problem in their head.

This directory exists to make us visible to each other. If you build across boundaries, you belong here.

Those who build across boundaries

Leonardo da Vinci historical

Painting · Engineering · Anatomy · Hydraulics · Optics

Artist, inventor, scientist. Designed flying machines, mapped the human body, painted the Mona Lisa, and engineered canal systems—seeing no boundary between observation, beauty, and mechanism.

1452 – 1519

Benjamin Franklin historical

Physics · Diplomacy · Publishing · Invention · Political Philosophy

Proved that lightning is electricity, founded the first public library, negotiated the French alliance, invented bifocals, and wrote prose sharp enough to start a revolution. Treated every problem as a prototype.

1706 – 1790

Ada Lovelace historical

Mathematics · Computing · Poetry · Music · Philosophy of Mind

Wrote the first computer program and, more importantly, saw that a computing machine could manipulate symbols of any kind—not just numbers. Called her approach "poetical science": the intersection of imagination and analysis.

1815 – 1852

Maya Okonkwo

Computational Neuroscience · Sculpture · Music Production

Builds neural network architectures inspired by the brain's visual cortex, then translates those patterns into large-scale steel sculptures. Releases ambient electronic albums that sonify neural spike trains. Postdoc at MIT, studio in Somerville.

b. 1991

Rafael Mendes

Architecture · Distributed Systems · Urban Agriculture

Designs buildings that grow food. Licensed architect who learned to code to build sensor networks for vertical farms embedded in facades. His firm in São Paulo ships both concrete and software.

b. 1987

Lena Johansson

Materials Science · Textile Design · Climate Policy

Developed a biodegradable polymer thread now used by three major fashion houses. Splits her time between a chemistry lab in Stockholm and advising the EU Commission on circular economy regulation. Weaves her own prototypes on a hand loom.

b. 1984

James Whitfield-Chen

Compiler Design · Calligraphy · Cognitive Science · Education

Built a programming language whose syntax is informed by Chinese calligraphic stroke order. Runs a school in Vancouver that teaches children to code, draw, and reason about formal systems simultaneously. Published in both ACM and a typography journal.

b. 1979

Priya Raghavan

Robotics · Bharatanatyam Dance · Philosophy of Embodiment

Builds soft robotics systems whose movement vocabularies are derived from classical Indian dance. Her PhD bridged the MIT Media Lab and the philosophy department. Currently leading a lab in Bangalore building prosthetics that move with human grace.

b. 1993
